My overaching interest is in how people seek out and process political (mis)information and news. In my current position at GESIS, I am studying negative campaigning on social media on the one hand, and try to advance methods of using web browsing data. Lately, I have become interested in the link of ecological change and political behaviour. I use data from online experiments, surveys, social media and trace data. Whatever I do, open science practices matter!

Work in progress

"Why Do Candidates 'Go Negative'? Unpacking the Mechanisms of Negative Campaigning" with Sebastian Stier (GESIS), Corinna Oschatz (University of Amsterdam), Alessandro Nai (University of Amsterdam), Jürgen Maier (RPTU Kaiserslautern-Landau) and Nora Kirkizh (TU Munich). Using a conjoint experiment with several hundreds of political candidates, we disentangle the different factors that may drive politicians to go negative.
Pre-registration

"Stimulus validity": Building on the working paper "Truth and Bias: Robust findings?" posted below, I argue that researchers in political science often design their studies around stimuli that are supposed to represent a larger universe of information, yet they rarely justify their selection of stimuli. I illustrate dangers of this lack of validity, and propose solutions.

"Statistical analysis of trace data: a guide", with Magdalena Wojcieszak (UC Davis), Ana Sofia Cardenal (Universitat Oberta de Catalunya), Sebastian Stier (GESIS), Andy Guess (Princeton), Ericka Menchen-Trevino (American University). In this paper, we are laying out some principles in the analysis of web browsing data, derived from our experience with ten browsing data sets from seven different countries and four research teams.

"Survey professionalism: New evidence from trace data", with Magdalena Wojcieszak (UC Davis), Jonathan Nagler (NYU), Tiago Ventura (NYU), Ericka Menchen-Trevino (American University). The extent to which survey respondents are "professional", that is, earn their living with surveys, has mostly been measured with surveys (!). We provide new evidence by exploring people's browsing data.

"Incentivizing news consumption with bots on Twitter", with Hadi Askari (UC Davis), Michael Heseltine (University of Amsterdam), Magdalena Wojcieszak (UC Davis), Ericka Menchen-Trevino (American University), Zubair Shafiq (UC Davis) and Anshuman Chabra (UC Davis). In this large-scale field experiment on Twitter, we test ways to get people to follow more high-quality news.

"Media Ideology in Multiparty Systems: Validated Scores for 16 European Countries" with Michael Heseltine (University of Amsterdam). Estimating media ideology with social media sharing patterns has so far been restricted to the US. We add to literature with data from Europe.

"Measuring outside search in survey experiments" with Fabio Torregiani (University of Milan): In many survey experiments related to political knowledge and misinformation, researchers do not control whether subjects "look up" the answer. We test ways to obtain subjects' browsing history with their consent, in order to measure outside search behaviour.
