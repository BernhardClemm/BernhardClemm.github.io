My primary research interest concerns people's factual beliefs about politics, and how citizens seek out, evaluate, remember and disseminate factual information and news. Related to this, I study how the characteristics of news sources shape these processes, and how information processing affects media trust and vice versa. I am further interested in how people's attitudes are socially influenced, and how people's social identities impact their political behaviour. I study these questions with online experiments, text-as-data methods, surveys and social media data.

Work in progress

"Assimilation bias, left and right": In this project, I point to the difficulty of comparing the strength of assimilation bias (the tendency to evaluate congruent facts more positively than incongruent facts) for different groups such as conservatives vs. liberals. I propose a design that samples factual information from news agency feeds, and controls for congruence of the information before testing people's assimilation bias in a survey experiment.

"Who falls for fake sources", with Paul Bauer (MZES University of Mannheim): In this paper, we build on our previous paper examining effects of sources on belief of news and want to find out who is susceptible to fake sources. We rely on newly developed methods by Athey and Imbens (2016) to find heterogenous treatment effects with machine learning.
