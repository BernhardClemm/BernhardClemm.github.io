I am interested in people's factual beliefs about politics, and how they seek out, evaluate, remember and disseminate factual information and news. Related to this, I study how the characteristics of news sources shape these processes, and how information processing affects media trust and vice versa. I also do research on the genetic sources of political behaviour. In my current research position at ASCoR, my focus is on the effects of media exposure on political and social polarization. I study these questions with data from online experiments, surveys, social media and people's browsing histories, using both conventional social-science statistics as well as machine learning methods. Further, I play around with ideas to improve data visualizations and take great interest in open science practice.

Work in progress

Media exposure and polarization with Magdalena Wojcieszak (UC Davis) and others: This project builds on a three-wave panel survey in three countries (US, Poland, the Netherlands) as well as participants' browsing behaviour in-between the waves. The panel structure of the data and the high-quality browsing data allows us to test causal effects of media exposure on a variety of outcomes, such as perceived polarization or attitude extremity.

"Does Source Credibility Moderate Persuasion? New Evidence from a Longitudinal Experiment" with Andy Guess (Princeton): Early scholarship in psychology proposed the idea that persuasion hinges on source credibility. However, existing studies almost universally takes source credibility as given. In contrast, we create a hypothetical news outlet and manipulate whether subjects see it as trustworthy, and later test whether greater source credibility boost persuasion. "Shareworthiness, source credibility and congruence" with Paul Bauer (MZES University of Mannheim): In this paper, we explore two factors that could influence people's sharing behaviour on social media: the credibility of the source and whether a news item's content is congruent with a respondent's attitudes. We find that congruence matters; sources make little difference, not even attenuating the bias towards sharing congruent content.

"Common genetic sources of political ideology and religiosity" with Chris Dawes (NYU): Previous studies have shown the contribution of genes to both relgiosity and ideology. But do these two traits have common genetic sources? We explore this question with the genetically informative German TwinLife data set.
